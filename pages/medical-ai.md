# Medical AI

The Medical AI direction is about how AI can support clinical work while staying careful about context, uncertainty, and human judgement.

I am especially interested in medical imaging and clinical decision support: how models interpret scans or visual evidence, how information is carried between stages of care, and how AI-generated outputs can help without encouraging overconfidence.

The core question is not simply whether a model can produce an answer. It is whether the system helps clinicians reason better, preserves important context, and makes uncertainty visible instead of hiding it behind a polished response.

## Current direction

- explore medical imaging as a future AI research area
- think about how models handle clinical context before, during, and after clinician decision points
- improve prompts and structured inputs so important information is not lost between steps
- reduce automation bias by keeping AI outputs clearly framed as support, not final authority
- investigate how uncertainty, limitations, and missing evidence should be communicated
- consider dataset provenance, bias, robustness, privacy, and safety from the start

## Why context matters

Clinical decisions happen in phases. Information can be gathered, summarised, transferred, interpreted, and acted on by different people or systems. At each stage, context can be lost.

One aim of this work is to think about how AI tools could preserve useful context across those stages: what was observed, what was uncertain, what was ruled out, what still needs checking, and what assumptions shaped the output.

## Ethical focus

Medical AI has to be built with a moral compass. A useful system should support better care, but it should also resist false certainty, avoid hiding bias, and make it easy for clinicians to question or override outputs.

The goal is not automation for its own sake. The goal is careful assistance: systems that improve reasoning, reduce avoidable errors, and respect the responsibility of clinical judgement.
