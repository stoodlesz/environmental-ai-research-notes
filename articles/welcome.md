# Environmental AI, Dune Systems, and Secure Machine Learning

Welcome to my research notes.

This space is where I am going to document a small side project at the intersection of environmental geography, artificial intelligence, and cybersecurity. I am interested in how modern AI tools can help us observe fragile ecosystems, but also in how we make those systems trustworthy: where the data comes from, how models behave, and what can go wrong when environmental decisions depend on machine learning.

The first focus is coastal sand dune ecosystems.

I've started a small side project that brings together two things I've always been fascinated by: environmental geography and AI.

When I was younger, I was particularly interested in coastal sand dune ecosystems and the concept of psammosere succession. Don't ask, I just found it really interesting. Psammosere succession is the gradual process where pioneer plants stabilise mobile sand and allow more complex ecosystems to develop over time. These systems are not just interesting biologically; they are also critical for natural coastal protection and biodiversity.

Recently, I started thinking about whether modern computer vision and satellite imagery could help monitor these environments more effectively.

## The project idea

The idea behind this project is to explore whether AI models can:

- identify stages of dune succession from satellite images
- detect ecosystem change over time, including loss or recovery of vegetation
- eventually simulate potential restoration scenarios based on vegetation patterns

Alongside the environmental side of the work, I'm also approaching this from a cybersecurity perspective. Environmental monitoring systems increasingly rely on machine learning and large datasets, but questions around data integrity, dataset provenance, and model robustness are rarely discussed in this space.

The goal is to build a small research pipeline that explores both sides: environmental AI and secure machine learning practices.

This will start with dune systems and psammoseres, but the same approach could potentially extend to wetlands, reforestation monitoring, and other ecosystem restoration efforts.

I'll be sharing progress as I build the dataset, train models, and test what these systems can actually detect from satellite imagery.
